Section 4 of 4 — Vigenère tab content

More structure: two-stage attack (key length, then per-column Caesar). One light formula (IC).

1 · What it is

Prose draft: The Vigenère cipher uses a short repeating keyword instead of a single shift. Each plaintext letter is shifted by the corresponding key letter (A = shift 0, B = shift 1, …). Because the key cycles, the same plaintext letter encrypts to different cipher letters depending on its position. For three centuries it was considered "the unbreakable cipher."

2 · How it works — tile the key

Prose: Write the key repeatedly above the plaintext, then add letter-by-letter (mod 26).

KEY    L E M O N L E M O N L E
PLAIN  A T T A C K A T D A W N
CIPHER L X F O P V E F R N H R

The plaintext letter A becomes L, X, E, N, R — different ciphertexts depending on where it sits.

3 · Why it's stronger — frequency analysis breaks

Prose: Because one plaintext letter maps to many ciphertext letters, the cipher's overall letter frequencies look flat — much closer to random than to English. A naive frequency attack finds nothing to align.

4 · How to crack it — two stages

Prose: Find the key length first; once known, the cipher decomposes into independent Caesars and falls.

Stage 1: Index of Coincidence. The IC measures how unevenly distributed the letters are. English text scores ~0.067; uniform random scores ~0.038. If we group every k-th cipher letter together (i.e. assume key length k), each group is a single Caesar shift of English when k is right — so its IC will be high. Try several k; the peak is the key length.

IC = Σᵢ nᵢ(nᵢ−1) ⁄ N(N−1) nᵢ = count of letter i; N = total letters in the group. Higher = more English-like.

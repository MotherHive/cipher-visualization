1 · What it is

Prose draft: A Caesar cipher is the simplest substitution cipher: every letter in the message is shifted by the same fixed number of positions in the alphabet. With a shift of 3, HELLO becomes KHOOR. Roman general Julius Caesar used a shift of 3 for his private correspondence — the trick is two thousand years old and trivial to break today.

2 · How it works — alphabet strips diagram

Prose: Lay two alphabets side by side and slide one against the other by the key. To encrypt, find your plaintext letter on top and read down. To decrypt, read up.

3 · Why it's weak — only 25 useful shifts

Prose: The key is a single number from 1 to 25. You can simply try every one and read down the column for the one that looks like English.

+1JGNNQ +2IFMMP +3HELLO ← +4GDKKN +5FCJJM …(20 more rows)

4 · How to crack it — frequency analysis

Prose: Even without trying all 25, the math gives you a shortcut. Letter frequencies in English are very uneven — E and T dominate. Count letter frequencies in the ciphertext, line them up against English, and the shift that makes them match is the key. Light formula: a chi-squared score over the 26 letters; minimum wins.
